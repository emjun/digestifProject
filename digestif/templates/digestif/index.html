digestif

[dee-zhe-steef] (noun)
a tool that supports scientific outreach and participant learning in online experiments

Researchers conduct large-scale online experiments for more diverse and large samples.

To do scientific outreach, iterate on experimental design, improve data quality, and attract more participants, researchers use digestif pages.

Digestif pages are at the end of online experiments and help participants learn and make sense of how they have just contributed to cutting edge research.

Explore our library of digestif pages from popular platforms, or create and download your own.

Marketing

Digestif pages that allow participants to share their personalized results lead to snowball recruitment.

Data Quality

The information promised on digestif pages can incentivize participants to contribute more honest data rather than game the study.

Feedback

The feedback participants give on digestif pages or give via email helps researchers identify technical bugs and language that is difficult to understand.

Debrief

Participants are curious about how what they do contributes to science. Researchers have an opportunity to explain the implications of their research on digestif pages.

Ethics

One way researchers can keep participants informed and respect their time and effort is to provide participants with information about the nature of the online study and research.

Outreach

Giving participants additional information on the digestif pages about research aims and findings is a form of scientific outreach that reaches a population different from other forms of outreach and online learning.

The Building Blocks of a Conclusion Page

All the blocks are optional. Researchers can choose based on their participants and goals.

Acknowledgments

Acknowledgement blocks thank the participant for their time and data contribution.

Experimental Design

Experimental design blocks tell participants why the online experiment was designed the way it was. These blocks can also explain how any personalized results were generated and the limitations of the study.

Feedback

Feedback blocks allow participants to provide suggestions, ideas, and concerns about the tasks. Based on the feedback, researchers can refine their studies iteratively.

Other Studies

Other studies blocks contain links to other studies on a platform, leveraging current traffic to attract more participation to other studies.

Personalized Results

Personalized results blocks provide give participants some insight into their performance/data. A common form of personalized results is to compare a participant's performance with the performance of other participants or groups already found in previous research (social comparison). The promise of personalized results is often used to advertise studies.

Previous Research

Previous research blocks allow researchers to share about their fields and previous research findings.

Research Goals

Research goals blocks explain how the results of the study will contribute to advancing various disciplines or improving applications (e.g., possible products, etc.).

Research Motivations

Research motivations blocks tell participants why data is being collected and/or the hypotheses that the study addresses.

Share

Share blocks allow participants to share their participation on social media, which can generate traffic for the study.

Community Building

Community building blocks enable participants to connect with researchers and researchers to build longer-term relationships with participants. Example block functionalities include joining a mailing list or liking a study platform on social media.
